Chapter One

Terminal, Shell, Prompt

Three different things, almost everyone gets them wrong.

Before any of tonight's tools make sense, you need to separate three things people lump together as "the terminal." They're stacked, like a phone call.

The terminal is the application — the window. iTerm2, Apple's Terminal.app, VS Code's integrated terminal. Its only job is to show characters and capture your keystrokes. It does no thinking.

The shell is the program running inside that window. It interprets what you type, runs commands, manages variables. On modern Macs that's zsh. The shell is the brain.

The prompt is the little bit of text the shell prints before each command — the part that says where you are, what branch you're on, that cloud icon for AWS. It's pure cosmetics, generated by the shell. Powerlevel10k and Starship are prompts. They aren't shells.

The terminal is the bar — the room where you're standing. The shell is the bartender taking your order. The prompt is the way they say "what'll it be?" — the phrasing, the eye contact. You can change the bar, the bartender, or the greeting, independently. Tonight you changed all three.

Once you internalize this, every tutorial online suddenly reads more clearly. "Add this to your shell" means ~/.zshrc. "Customize your prompt" means Starship config. "Try a new terminal" means installing iTerm2. Different layers, different files.

Chapter Two

Oh My Zsh

A framework that organizes your shell's wardrobe.

Oh My Zsh is the most-installed thing in this whole stack and the most misunderstood. People say "I installed Oh My Zsh" as if they installed a new shell. They didn't. Zsh was already there. Oh My Zsh just bolted a tidy management system on top of it.

Zsh on its own is powerful but unopinionated — it has no built-in way to load themes, manage plugins, or share configs between machines. Oh My Zsh adds three conventions: a folder structure (~/.oh-my-zsh/), a plugins system (the plugins=(...) array in .zshrc), and a themes system (ZSH_THEME=).

Zsh is a kitchen. Oh My Zsh is the line of labeled containers, the pegboard for tools, and the whiteboard schedule. The kitchen still cooks the food — but now you can find the salt and a new chef can read the prep list. None of it changes the recipes themselves.

This is why removing Oh My Zsh doesn't break your shell — your shell was always zsh. And it's why installing OMZ is fundamentally just cloning a folder and sourcing a script from .zshrc. Everything else — the git aliases, the colored prompts, the plugin loading — is just convention layered on top.

Chapter Three

The Two Plugins

Autosuggestions and syntax-highlighting, demystified.

You added two plugins early in the evening. They feel like magic but each does exactly one thing.

zsh-autosuggestions watches your command history. As you type, it shows the rest of the most recent matching command in grey. Press → to accept it. That's the whole feature. It doesn't predict the future or use AI; it just remembers what you've already typed and shows you the closest match.

zsh-syntax-highlighting looks at each character as you type and asks: is this a real command? Real commands turn green. Typos turn red. Strings get their own color. It's a live linter for your command line — the same idea as syntax highlighting in VS Code, just running in your shell.

Autosuggestions is the experienced bartender finishing your sentence: "the usual? Negroni, slightly less Campari?" Syntax-highlighting is the spell-check underline that appears as you write — green when the word exists, red when you've fat-fingered it. Both are quietly working, neither demands attention.

You loaded them by adding their names to the plugins=(...) array in .zshrc. That array is read once when zsh starts, and Oh My Zsh sources each plugin's script. Order matters for one of them — syntax-highlighting must be last in the list, because it patches into zsh's keystroke handler and any plugin loaded after it could break the patching.

Chapter Four

The Prompt: Starship

Why we walked away from Powerlevel10k.

Powerlevel10k was the king of zsh prompts for years — fast, info-dense, beautiful. Then in 2024 its author quietly stepped back, marking the project "on life support." It still works. It just won't get fixes or new features.

Starship is the modern alternative the community has converged on. It's written in Rust (read: blazing fast), it works with any shell — zsh, bash, fish, PowerShell, Nushell — and it's actively maintained by a real team, not one heroic individual. Crucially, the configuration is one file (~/.config/starship.toml) that you can copy between machines without touching anything else.

The way Starship plugs in is also cleaner. A single line at the end of .zshrc:

# ── activate Starship as the prompt ──
eval "$(starship init zsh)"

That eval takes the output of starship init zsh (which is a chunk of zsh code) and runs it. The result is that every time zsh asks "what should the prompt look like right now?", Starship answers based on your current directory, git branch, language version, AWS context, and so on. It's smart enough to only show segments that are relevant — which is why your AWS region disappears when no AWS env vars are set, and your git branch only shows in repos.

Powerlevel10k was a finely tailored suit by a master who's retired. It still fits, but no one's altering it anymore. Starship is the same suit cut by an active atelier with a workshop full of apprentices. Both look sharp; only one will be there to fix the seam in two years.

Chapter Five

Nerd Fonts & The Glyph Problem

Why your terminal needed a special font to show icons.

A font is a collection of glyphs — drawings that the computer renders when it encounters specific character codes. Regular fonts include letters, numbers, punctuation, and a smattering of symbols. They don't include git branch icons, cloud symbols, or folder pictograms, because no standard alphabet does.

When Starship tries to print a git branch icon and your font has no glyph for that character code, you see a tiny empty box. It's not a bug — the font is honestly telling you "I don't know what this is."

Nerd Fonts are normal coding fonts (Meslo, Fira Code, JetBrains Mono, Hack, etc.) that have been "patched" to include thousands of additional symbols: file-type icons, branch icons, language logos, weather symbols. The "NF" you see in font names — MesloLGS NF — stands for "Nerd Font."

Imagine writing a letter that includes a Chinese character. If the recipient's printer only has English type, that character won't print at all — it'll appear as an empty rectangle. Not because the printer is broken, but because the type tray is missing the right slug. A Nerd Font is a type tray with thousands of extra slugs added.

This is why the same prompt looks gorgeous in iTerm with MesloLGS NF and like a row of empty boxes in a default Terminal.app session — the prompt is generating the same character codes either way; only the font knows how to draw them.

Chapter Six

The Modern CLI Trio

zoxide, eza, fzf — the new replacements for tools you didn't know were old.

The classic Unix commands — cd, ls, grep, find — were designed in the 1970s for terminals that displayed monochrome text on glass screens. They still work fine, but a generation of developers has built friendlier versions in Rust and Go. The three you installed are the most beloved.

The Trio

zoxide
A smarter cd. It remembers every folder you've ever visited and lets you jump back to any of them with a fragment of the name (z week2). The interactive picker (zi) shows them all in a fuzzy menu.
eza
A modern ls. Same job, but with file-type icons (using your Nerd Font), color coding, git status per file, and a built-in tree view.
fzf
A general-purpose fuzzy finder. It pipes any list — files, folders, history, processes — through an interactive search interface. The shell integration gives you Ctrl+R for fuzzy history, Ctrl+T for fuzzy file picking, Option+C for fuzzy directory hopping.

The classic commands are paper Rolodexes — they work, you flip through them, you find the card. The trio is a search bar with autocomplete. Same data, an entirely different speed of thought. After a week with Option+C and zi, typing cd ../../some/long/path feels archaic.

The deeper lesson here is the Unix philosophy in action: each of these tools does one thing. fzf doesn't know about your filesystem; it just sorts and filters lines. The shell integration scripts are what pipe folder names into fzf and pipe the selection out as a cd argument. Small composable parts, glued together by your shell.

The shell isn't a tool you use. It's a tiny programming language you live inside.
Chapter Seven

Aliases, Functions, Symlinks

Three forms of indirection — the shell's most powerful idea.

An alias is a nickname. alias ll="eza --icons --git -l" tells the shell: when I type ll, pretend I typed the longer thing. Aliases can't take logic, conditions, or arguments — they're text substitution, nothing more.

A function is an alias with a brain. It can accept arguments, run multiple commands, branch on conditions. Your dotsave is a function because we wanted it to take an optional commit message:

dotsave() {
  cd ~/dotfiles && brew bundle dump --force && git add . && \
  git commit -m "${1:-update}" && git push
}

That ${1:-update} is shell shorthand for "use the first argument; if there isn't one, use the literal word 'update' instead." A pattern you'll see in scripts forever.

A symlink is a different kind of indirection — at the filesystem level instead of the command level. It's a file that says nothing except "the real thing is over there." When you ran:

❯ ln -s ~/dotfiles/.zshrc ~/.zshrc

...you created a tiny pointer file at ~/.zshrc whose only contents are the path ~/dotfiles/.zshrc. When zsh opens its config, the operating system silently follows the pointer and reads the real file in the dotfiles repo. Zsh has no idea anything's unusual. It just works.

Aliases are nicknames at a regulars' bar — say "the usual" and the bartender pours your drink. Functions are nicknames with options — "the usual, but with mezcal tonight." Symlinks are something else entirely: imagine moving the wine cellar to a warehouse across town and putting a sign in the cellar that says "see warehouse." The bartender keeps reaching for the shelf, the sign quietly redirects them, the wine still arrives.

Indirection is one of programming's deepest ideas. It lets you separate what something is called from where it actually lives — which is what made tonight's dotfiles repo possible.

Chapter Eight

Dotfiles & The Brewfile

Treating your environment as code.

Files in your home directory whose names start with a dot (.zshrc, .gitconfig, .config/) are conventionally hidden from regular file listings. Developers call them dotfiles. They're the configuration files that, taken together, define your personal computing environment.

A dotfiles repo is the practice of putting those files in a git repository, usually pushed to GitHub. Three benefits stack up:

First, history. Every change is timestamped and reversible. When you change something today and your shell breaks tomorrow, git diff shows exactly what shifted. No more "I have no idea what I last touched."

Second, portability. New laptop? Clone the repo, symlink the files into place, and your environment lives again. The Brewfile (generated by brew bundle dump) extends this to your installed software — one command (brew bundle install) reinstalls every package, cask, and VS Code extension you had.

Third, and quietly the most important: understanding. Once your config is a tracked artifact in git, you start treating it as code — something to refactor, document, and improve. You stop hoarding settings and start curating them.

Most people's environments are a kitchen built up over years — every drawer holds something, no one remembers when it arrived. A dotfiles repo is the same kitchen, but with a written floorplan, a printed inventory, and a ledger of changes. Burn it down, and you can rebuild it identically by Friday.

You also added dotsave — a one-word workflow for saving and pushing changes. That detail matters more than it looks. Tools you have to remember to use, you eventually stop using. Tools that fit into a single word, you keep using forever.

The Mental Model You Built Tonight

You now have, layered from the bottom up: a terminal (iTerm2) that draws characters; a shell (zsh) that runs commands; a framework (Oh My Zsh) that organizes the shell's plugins and configuration; plugins (autosuggestions, syntax-highlighting) that make typing faster and safer; a prompt (Starship) that draws the smart contextual line at every command; a font (MesloLGS NF) that knows how to draw the icons that prompt produces; modern CLI tools (zoxide, eza, fzf) that replace the slow legacy commands; aliases and functions that condense your habits into single words; and a dotfiles repo that backs the whole arrangement up to GitHub, version-controlled, restorable.

None of it was random. Every piece you installed answers a specific need that the previous piece exposed. Once you can see the layers, you'll be able to debug any of them — and pick replacements with intention rather than tutorials.

Now — the recipe is yours. Go cook. ✦
